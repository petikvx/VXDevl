Evaluating Anti-Virus Software for Small Businesses
by Paul Schmehl
last updated March 26, 2001

Many small businesses today have experienced at least one virus outbreak. If you haven't been infected by Melissa or Loveletter, you've at least had to deal with the flood of e-mail that your vendors and correspondents may have sent you as a result, not to mention the questions and warning notices from employees and friends.

By now you should already be using anti-virus software, but how do you know if what you've bought is optimal for your needs? If you need to renew your license, should you try a new vendor? If you've never purchased anti-virus software before, how do you know what to buy? This article will attempt to offer readers some guidelines to help them buy the virus protection that is most appropriate for their small business. In addition to advising readers on what to look for in anti-virus software, this discussion will also provide some resources that readers can use to evaluate the various products that are available.

Matching Anti-Virus Software to the Company's Needs

Before you update your existing anti-virus software licenses or buy new software, you should consider how your company will use the software. If your business requires the constant exchange of Word documents, then excellent Macro virus protection is crucial. If you exchange many different types of e-mail attachments, protection against scripted worms and other exploits may be more important. If you do neither of these things in quantity, good general protection may be sufficient and ease of use may become a more important consideration. As this discussion proceeds, we will point out some of the ways in which it is important to evaluate anti-virus software in the context of the organization's particular needs.

Matching Anti-Virus Software to the Company's Capabilities

In order to protect your business effectively, your anti-virus software should be easy to install, configure, and manage, and it should fit with the technological expertise of the organization's administrative staff. The world's greatest anti-virus detection is of little use if you can't figure out how to deploy it, if updates are difficult to implement, if management of the software is a time consuming chore or the software doesn't provide protection for the way your business uses its computers.

This aspect is particularly relevant to small enterprises that may not have the resources to devote to a full-time, highly-qualified security administrator. In organizations of twenty people or less, this position may be filled on a part-time basis by someone who does not have any technical training or experience. In cases such as this, it is imperative for the security of the organization that the anti-virus software that is purchased is suitable to the expertise of the administrator. It is also imperative that the software fits the network's users. If the employees find it intrusive or think it slows down their computer, they'll probably disable it. Then all the software in the world won't protect you from an outbreak.

On the other hand, software that's a snap to manage and doesn't require a lot of configuration is hardly worth buying if it misses viruses, and you spend more time cleaning up the mess than you spent on the software. Striking a balance between ease of use and protection isn't easy, and the information about which anti-virus software is the "best" can often be confusing.

Resources for Evaluating Anti-Virus Software

So how do you evaluate anti-virus software carefully before you buy? Is there such a thing as "best of breed" anti-virus software? How do you wade through the marketing claims of the anti-virus companies and get to the bottom line - protection, ease of use and non-intrusiveness?

Magazine Product Reviews

Anti-virus software can be evaluated regularly in several ways. PC magazines, such as ZDNet's PC Magazine, routinely evaluate the major vendors, both in product reviews and in side-by-side product comparisons. These reviews often provide useful information, such as the price per unit, ease of use ratings and overall product ratings. Unfortunately, they don't tell the whole story. They generally use relatively small virus collections to test the software, and they seldom reveal the qualifications of the test lab staff. They also don't test the full range of products that are available.

TruSecure Certification

There are also online services that evaluate a-v software. One good source for product evaluations is TruSecure Corporation (formerly ICSA.net.) According to their web site's "About Us" link, "The ICSA Labs sets standards, performs research, tracks and measures risks, and certifies 98 percent of the market's anti-virus software." ICSA.net has been certifying anti-virus software for 11 years. They certify anti-virus products in a number of categories, including On-Demand/On-Access, Cleaning, Internet Gateways, Microsoft Exchange Gateways, Lotus Notes Gateways, ASPs, ISPs and On-Line Scanners. (To view a list of products that TruSecure has certified, click here.)

Once a product has been certified, TruSecure tests it every three months, without prior notification to the vendor, to assure ongoing compliance. If a vendor's product fails the certification check, TruSecure will notify the vendor and give them a seven-day window to correct the defect and publish it on their web site. Otherwise, they will lose their certification, and they will have to publish a major version update (rather than a quick fix) to be certified again.

An ICSA Labs certification indicates that an anti-virus product offers the following:

  • 100% detection of the viruses listed in the current in-the-wild list;
  • 100% detection of the viruses in the ICSA Common Infectors Test Suite;
  • 100% detection of ICSA's Polymorphic Test Suite;
  • 90% detection of macro viruses in the ICSA Virus Collection;
  • 90% detection of the ICSA Virus Collection; and,
  • zero false alarms.

As you can see, a product that achieves ICSA certification has met some fairly stringent criteria for performance. If a product is ICSA certified, you can probably depend upon that product to protect your business from the devastating effects of a virus outbreak.

This list indicates the necessity to understand the particular anti-virus needs of your organization. For instance, if your business needs excellent Macro protection, you will need to examine the product evaluations carefully, 90% detection will not be good enough.

Virus Bulletin

Another evaluation process that is even more stringent than ICSA's; the Virus Bulletin 100% awards. The VB 100% award is the technophile's equivalent of the Oscar. In order to win a VB 100% award, an anti-virus product must not fail to detect a single in-the-wild virus. (An "in the wild" virus is one that has been detected in general circulation, outside of a controlled laboratory environment by someone who reports that detection to the "official" Wild List.)

The Virus Bulletin awards the prestigious VB 100% award bi-monthly, testing products on different platforms every other month. These include Windows ME, Windows 98, Windows NT, Novell Netware 5.1, and DOS. To view a helpful summary chart that reveals a product's performance over the past two years is available, to view it, click here.

When you study the results at the Virus Bulletin web site, you should notice a pattern. Some products that have done well in the past may not have done particularly well recently. Other products that haven't done well in the past have done quite well recently. What this means is a source of constant argument among virus experts. Some commentators contend that if a product misses one single virus, it is not worth using. (They frequently change their tune when their personal favorite fails to make the grade.) Others argue that one must examine the testing criteria and the results carefully to determine the cause of the failure before making a determination about the meaning of the tests.

Adding to the confusion is the fact that some vendors, even ones offering ICSA certified products, don't submit their products to the Virus Bulletin for testing. It isn't possible to say with certainty why any particular vendor failed to submit a product for testing. If you're interested in finding out more, the cost of a subscription to the Virus Bulletin is $395 US annually. While this may be outside the budget of some small businesses, others may find it worthwhile to have the details of each test at hand when making buying decisions.

AV-Test.Org

Another entry to the anti-virus testing field is AV-Test.org, a cooperative effort between an IT consulting firm and the Business Information Workgroup at the Institute of Technical and Business Information Systems at the Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg. Their test results are downloadable and can be viewed on line also, in the form of a spreadsheet. The results are comprehensive, but they can be a bit difficult to follow because of the format. Keep in mind also that many of the products tested are the German versions, and they may not be fully comparable to the version you are using.

Evaluating Anti-Virus Software for the Company's Needs

We referred earlier to the need to take your company's needs into account, and the online resources can help in this respect. For instance, if an on-access scanner fails to detect every boot virus, should you strike it from your list of potential purchases? Boot viruses are rare today, and they are usually transmitted by floppy disk. If you don't exchange many floppy disks in your business, the likelihood of contracting a boot virus is much less than it would be if you exchanged floppies routinely.

On the other hand, if a scanner does a poor job of detecting Visual Basic viruses (like Loveletter) and you conduct business routinely by e-mail, you may want to think twice about using that scanner, even if you've used it in the past without incident. This is particularly true if your company has chosen Outlook or Outlook Express as your e-mail client, because these viruses tend to target the Microsoft mail clients. The decision is even more critical if you use Microsoft Exchange as your mail server platform.

Evaluating Anti-Virus Software for Ease of Use

Once you've determined the competency of various vendors' products, you should consider the user and management interfaces. How easy is the software to use? How difficult is it to manage? These are questions that are not easily answered. Outside the magazine product reviews, which provide some assistance, there are no competent testing sites for interface review. Here you are essentially on your own.

Anti-virus product vendors have only recently begun focusing on improvement of the management of their products. Depending on the size of your business, it may be easier to install and update the software by hand than it is to use the management interface. (It may also be much cheaper.) However, there is a point at which manual installation and updating is cost prohibitive. Then the management interface can make or break your efforts to protect yourself. Surprisingly, some vendor's management interfaces are significantly easier to use in small businesses than they are in large enterprises.

Evaluating Anti-Virus Through Free Trial Periods

Many vendors make their software available for download on a trial basis. During the evaluation phase, you should take advantage of this to test the product's interface and operating characteristics. Here you are not concerned about detection or cleaning capability. You simply want to know how easy the product is to use and update. What are the "gotchas" that will haunt you later on? To some degree, this is a matter of personal taste, so you should ask several people to try the software out. Set a specific time frame for the trial and ask them to give you their evaluation of the interface. Ask them if there was anything that made it difficult to use or understand. Did it interfere with their work? Was there anything they found particularly annoying about it?

This evaluation should only be done once you've narrowed your choice of software to three or at the most four products. These should be products which professional testing shows will provide the protection you need for your specific uses. At this point, your goal shouldn't be to choose the "best" product but the most effective one for your business. As a small business owner, you cannot afford to select a product that your employees won't use, regardless of its capabilities.

Evaluating Anti-Virus Updates

The recent outbreak of the "Anna Kournikova" virus revealed weaknesses in some vendors' detection schemes while other vendors had detection in place before the virus was released. Even the best detection capability and a fine management interface is useless if the software isn't used or updated properly. Configuring and updating the software is just as important as its capabilities, perhaps even more so.

During this "usefulness" evaluation, you should look at how the software is updated, because updating is critical to obtaining protection against newly discovered viruses. Can you download the updates to your network and update all the clients from there? Or will you have to depend upon the vagaries of Internet connectivity to get updates when they are critical? Recent virus outbreaks have shown that getting updates can be very problematic when a virus outbreak has occurred, as networks all over the world are trying to update at the same time. Some vendors have taken steps to alleviate the problem by providing alternate download sites. Check to see if the ones you are evaluating offer this service. It may prove critical in a crisis situation.

Check to see if the vendor offers "emergency" updates. If they do, how easy are they to install? Some vendors offer daily updates, others offer weekly updates. Recent experience has shown that the fast pace of updating may be detrimental. Some vendors have had to recall updates after releasing them. Others have had to rush new updates out to correct a problem with previous ones.

Evaluating Anti-Virus Software by Price

Once you've assessed the competency of a product and evaluated its usefulness, the last thing to consider is price. At this point, the products you've selected should be comparable enough that price could be the deciding factor. Does a vendor have incentives for volume purchasing? Does anyone have a special offer that would save your company money? What will it cost to renew in the future? How often does the vendor change versions? Do the versions require the purchase of new licenses? Or will your present license cover updates for a specified period of time?

The End of the Beginning

After you've evaluated a product's competency, usability and price, you can purchase with the confidence that you will be protected from virus attacks both now and in the future, because you've shopped prudently and chosen wisely. However, purchasing the anti-virus software is not the end of the process; rather, as Winston Churchill once stated, it is only the end of the beginning.

Once the new anti-virus software has been researched, purchased and installed, it is still necessary to keep your software up to date. Furthermore, because an organization's computers are interconnected, it is vital that all people who are working on the network are aware of the risks that viruses pose. As such, it is crucial that ongoing user education is implemented to make sure that the networks users are not jeopardizing the protection that the anti-virus software provides. Users must be made aware of the software and how to operate it when required, for scanning floppy disks or e-mail attachments, for instance. As alluded to earlier, they must be made aware of the importance of the software so that they don't disable it or make it otherwise inoperable.

While the process of evaluating, purchasing and administering anti-virus software may seem daunting to the small business operator, think of how much trouble it may save in the long run. Once an effective, appropriate program is in place and regular updates are scheduled, the organization can get on with the many other challenges of running a small business, secure in the knowledge that their network and the valuable information that resides on it is as safe as possible from virus infection.
